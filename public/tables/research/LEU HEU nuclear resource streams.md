# Nuclear Power Resource Streams

## Primary Inputs

The dominant commodity input is **uranium**, which passes through a long supply chain before reaching the reactor. The upstream steps each involve commodity-like materials: uranium ore concentrate (yellowcake, U₃O₈), converted uranium hexafluoride (UF₆), enriched UF₆, and finally fabricated fuel assemblies (typically UO₂ ceramic pellets clad in zirconium alloy). The enrichment step is really a service measured in separative work units (SWU), but it effectively splits the uranium feed into two streams — a smaller enriched product and a larger depleted uranium (DU) tails stream.

Beyond fuel, the other major commodity input is **water**, used both as a neutron moderator (in thermal reactors) and as the primary coolant and heat transfer medium. Various **chemicals** are consumed in smaller quantities for water chemistry control, including boric acid (in PWRs, used as a soluble neutron absorber), lithium hydroxide, hydrazine, and ion exchange resins. **Zirconium alloy** is consumed as fuel cladding and structural material in fuel assemblies, though it is loaded on a cycle basis rather than continuously. Control rods use materials like silver-indium-cadmium alloys or boron carbide, but these are long-lived components rather than consumables.

## Categories of Byproducts

Nuclear byproducts fall into several broad categories. **Spent nuclear fuel** is the most significant — it is highly radioactive and contains residual uranium (~95% of the original heavy metal), plutonium and other transuranic actinides (~1%), and fission products (~4%, including cesium-137, strontium-90, iodine-129, noble gases, and many others). If the fuel is reprocessed, these constituents are separated into distinct waste and product streams; if not, spent fuel is managed as intact high-level waste.

**Operational radioactive wastes** are generated in lower-level but larger-volume streams. These include low-level waste (contaminated clothing, tools, filters, spent ion exchange resins) and intermediate-level waste (activated reactor internals, chemical sludges, and concentrated liquid wastes). **Gaseous and liquid effluents** are released in controlled, very small quantities after treatment — these include tritium, carbon-14, noble gases like krypton-85 and xenon-133, and trace amounts of particulate activity. **Waste heat** is the largest byproduct by sheer magnitude: a light water reactor operates at roughly 33% thermal efficiency, meaning about two units of thermal energy are rejected for every unit of electricity produced.

Upstream of the reactor, **depleted uranium** is the major byproduct of enrichment, and **mill tailings** (large volumes of low-activity sandy residue) are produced at the mining and milling stage.

## Rough Quantities per 1 MWe-hour

The following figures assume continuous operation at 1 MWe output and are normalized to a per-hour basis. These are order-of-magnitude estimates to give a sense of scale.

**Typical LEU plant (commercial LWR, fuel enriched to ~4–5% U-235):**

For inputs, the natural uranium requirement is roughly 20–25 grams per hour per MWe, accounting for the full supply chain from mine to fuel fabrication (this corresponds to about 170–200 tonnes of natural uranium per year for a 1000 MWe plant at 90% capacity factor). The actual enriched fuel consumed (burned up and discharged) is about 2–3 grams per hour per MWe. The enrichment process requires roughly 5–6 SWU per hour per MWe. Cooling water flow depends enormously on the cooling system design: a once-through cooled plant circulates on the order of 100–200 tonnes per hour per MWe through its condensers, while a cooling-tower plant evaporates roughly 1–2 tonnes per hour per MWe. Chemical consumables (boric acid, treatment chemicals, resins) are on the order of a few grams per hour per MWe collectively — small compared to everything else.

For byproducts, spent fuel is produced at roughly 2–3 grams per hour per MWe (essentially equal in mass to the fuel consumed, since mass is nearly conserved — fission only converts a tiny fraction to energy). Depleted uranium tails from enrichment amount to roughly 17–22 grams per hour per MWe — this is the largest solid material byproduct by mass in the fuel cycle. Waste heat rejection is about 2 MWth per MWe (for a 33% efficient plant), which translates to roughly 7 GJ per hour. Operational low- and intermediate-level waste is on the order of 5–15 grams per hour per MWe (a rough average; this varies widely by plant). Gaseous and liquid radioactive releases are negligible in mass terms — micrograms per hour — though they are significant from a radiological monitoring standpoint.

**HEU-fueled systems (research reactors, naval propulsion — typically 20–93% enrichment):**

HEU-fueled reactors are not used for commercial electricity generation, so mapping them to "1 MWe" requires some translation. These reactors are generally much smaller (a few MWe to perhaps 100–200 MWe for a naval propulsion reactor), and many research reactors produce no electricity at all. The key differences in the resource stream are as follows. Because the fuel is much more highly enriched, far less fuel mass is needed per unit of energy — a naval reactor core might operate for 10–30 years without refueling. The fuel consumption rate might be on the order of 0.5–1 gram per hour per MWe or even less, depending on burnup achieved. However, the natural uranium and SWU demand per kilogram of HEU fuel produced is vastly higher: producing 1 kg of 93%-enriched uranium requires roughly 180–250 kg of natural uranium feed and around 200+ SWU, compared to about 8–10 kg of natural feed and 4–5 SWU for 1 kg of LEU. So the natural uranium input per MWe-hour may be comparable to or somewhat lower than LEU plants (because HEU fuel achieves higher burnup), but the enrichment work per MWe-hour is significantly higher. Depleted uranium tails production is correspondingly much larger per kilogram of fuel produced, though this is partially offset by the smaller fuel mass. Spent fuel volumes are very small in mass terms but are more highly enriched in residual fissile material and can contain weapons-usable quantities of uranium, which is the central nonproliferation concern with HEU fuel cycles. Waste heat per MWe depends on the reactor's thermal efficiency — naval reactors may operate at 20–25% efficiency due to compact design constraints, meaning somewhat more waste heat per MWe than a commercial plant.

## Summary Perspective

The striking feature of nuclear power is how tiny the fuel and waste mass flows are compared to any combustion-based energy source. A coal plant producing 1 MWe burns roughly 400–500 kg of coal per hour and produces a comparable mass of CO₂ plus tens of kilograms of ash. A nuclear plant producing 1 MWe consumes a few grams of uranium fuel per hour and produces a few grams of spent fuel. The tradeoff is that those few grams are intensely radioactive and require sophisticated long-term management, and the upstream enrichment infrastructure is complex, energy-intensive, and proliferation-sensitive — especially for HEU fuel cycles.
