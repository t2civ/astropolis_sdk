## Recommended Depth Ranges for Your Five Strata

The depth boundaries I'd recommend are driven by a combination of current extraction technology limits, geomechanical transitions, and thermal constraints. Here's my reasoning for each:

**Surface: 0 – 0.3 km (0 – 300 m)**

This encompasses virtually all quarrying, strip mining, and the majority of open-pit mining operations globally. Most open pits are shallower than 300 m. The notable exception is Bingham Canyon (Utah, USA), which reaches roughly 1.2 km in depth, and a few "super pits" in Western Australia that reach 500–600 m — but these are extreme outliers. Strip mining overburden is almost always less than 60 m. This stratum also covers shallow water wells, shallow coal-seam gas wells, and surface-level brine extraction. Regolith and stone are overwhelmingly sourced from this layer. For your "surface drilling" abstraction, this would cover wells targeting reservoirs within 300 m of the surface, which includes many water wells, some shallow oil seeps, and shallow coal-seam methane. This boundary is the most debatable of the five; I discuss an alternative below.

**Subsurface: 0.3 – 2.0 km**

This is the workhorse stratum for conventional underground mining and routine drilling. The vast majority of underground metal mines globally operate within this range: iron ore, copper, gold, uranium, and industrial metals. Most coal underground mining occurs at the shallower end (300–800 m). Conventional oil and gas reservoirs in many mature basins (e.g., parts of the Permian Basin, North Sea, and the Middle East) have target depths in this range. This stratum is well within the thermal comfort zone for mining — at 2 km depth, temperatures are typically 60–80°C given a continental geothermal gradient of roughly 25–30°C/km (Turcotte and Schubert, *Geodynamics*, Cambridge University Press), which is manageable with ventilation and cooling but marks the beginning of significant thermal challenges.

**Deep: 2.0 – 5.0 km**

This stratum spans from the depth of routine underground mining up to the approximate limit of current mining technology. The deepest mines in the world — Mponeng and the former TauTona mine in South Africa — operate at roughly 3.5–4.0 km depth, where rock-face temperatures reach 60–66°C and extensive refrigeration is required (Ranjith et al., 2017, *Journal of Rock Mechanics and Geotechnical Engineering*). For drilling, this range is entirely routine; conventional deep oil and gas wells commonly target 2–5 km. Many shale plays (e.g., Marcellus, Eagle Ford) have target depths in the 1.5–4 km range. At 5 km, temperatures are roughly 150–170°C, and lithostatic pressure is approximately 130–140 MPa — challenging but well within current drilling capability.

**Extreme Deep: 5.0 – 10.0 km**

This stratum is beyond current mining capability but within the reach of advanced drilling. Present-day ultra-deep wells routinely operate in the 5–8 km range, and some frontier wells push toward 10 km. The Kola Superdeep Borehole (Kola Peninsula, Russia, drilled 1970–1992) reached 12.262 km measured depth but encountered only ~180°C because of the anomalously low geothermal gradient of that specific shield setting. In a more typical continental geotherm, 10 km corresponds to roughly 270–320°C, which is above the supercritical point of water (374°C at surface pressure, but lower critical temperature in the presence of dissolved salts and at high confining pressure — this is a complex thermodynamic regime). Resources in this stratum for your simulation would predominantly be hydrocarbons (deep gas, deep oil, condensates) and possibly deep brine systems carrying dissolved minerals such as lithium. In the speculative portion of your simulation, future mining technologies penetrating this stratum would need to address massive rock pressure, high temperature, and the transition from brittle to semi-ductile rock behavior.

**Ultra Deep: 10.0 – 20.0 km**

This stratum is almost entirely speculative from an extraction standpoint. Only a handful of boreholes have ever exceeded 10 km, and none have been production wells at that depth. The Kola borehole and the Z-44 Chayvo well (Sakhalin, Russia, ~12.376 km measured depth) represent the current frontier. The brittle-ductile transition in continental crust typically occurs somewhere in the 10–20 km range, depending on crustal composition, geotherm, and strain rate (Kohlstedt et al., 1995, *Journal of Geophysical Research*). Below this transition, rock behaves plastically, which fundamentally changes the nature of excavation — boreholes tend to close, cavities are unstable, and conventional mining concepts break down. Temperatures at 15–20 km are roughly 400–600°C. Resources at these depths are highly speculative: deep abiogenic methane (the deep gas hypothesis, though its economic significance remains debated — see Gold, 1992, *The Deep Hot Biosphere*), deep metal-bearing hydrothermal systems, and potentially magmatic-associated mineral concentrations. I'd recommend capping this stratum at 20 km rather than extending to the Moho (30–50 km under continents) because even in a speculative simulation, extraction below the brittle-ductile transition and at temperatures exceeding 500–600°C is extremely difficult to justify as physically plausible with any extrapolation of known engineering principles.

## Alternative: A Six-Stratum Model

One concern with the five-stratum model is that the surface stratum (0–0.3 km) is asked to do a lot of work. In practice, there is a meaningful distinction between truly surficial extraction (quarrying, strip mining, placer mining, where you are essentially scraping the surface) and large-scale open-pit mining that involves removing hundreds of meters of overburden. A six-stratum alternative would split the surface into two layers:

The strata would be: **Surface** at 0–0.1 km (quarrying, strip mining, regolith extraction, placer deposits, shallow wells), **Near-Surface** at 0.1–0.5 km (open-pit mining including large pits, shallow underground mining, shallow drilling), **Subsurface** at 0.5–2.0 km (conventional underground mining, standard drilling), **Deep** at 2.0–5.0 km (deep mining approaching current limits, routine deep drilling), **Extreme Deep** at 5.0–12.0 km (advanced drilling at or near the current technological frontier, speculative future mining), and **Ultra Deep** at 12.0–20.0 km (fully speculative, beyond all current extraction). The advantage of this scheme is that the extreme deep boundary at 12 km aligns closely with the actual demonstrated borehole depth limit, giving you a clean demarcation between "technologically achievable today, if barely" and "requires future technology."

The disadvantage is additional model complexity. Whether the added granularity is worth it depends on how differently you want to parameterize cost, energy, and technology access within the top 500 m.

## Flags and Concerns

There are several points about your design that I think are worth raising:

**Your resource list has some notable omissions.** Geothermal heat is a major energy resource extracted from depth, and it maps very naturally onto your strata (shallow geothermal at subsurface, deep conventional geothermal at deep, enhanced/supercritical geothermal at extreme deep and ultra deep). Lithium-bearing brines are increasingly important and are extracted by drilling and pumping; they don't fit cleanly into your "rare earths ore" or "industrial metals ore" categories. Sand and gravel (construction aggregates) are among the highest-volume extracted materials on Earth by mass — they could be folded into "regolith" or "industrial minerals," but it's worth being intentional about this. Finally, water itself (fresh groundwater, geothermal fluids) might warrant inclusion depending on your simulation's scope.

**The abstraction is not too oversimplified for a simulation, but you should be aware of what it hides.** The single biggest hidden variable is lateral geological variation. A given stratum at 1 km depth in a sedimentary basin (say, the Gulf Coast) hosts a completely different resource profile than the same depth in a Precambrian shield (say, the Canadian Shield). Sedimentary basins are where hydrocarbons live; shield and orogenic terranes are where hard-rock mineral deposits concentrate. Your simulation will need some mechanism — perhaps regional geological "types" overlaid on the strata — to differentiate these settings. Without this, you'll get unrealistic results like finding oil in Archean granite.

**The near-offshore integration is trickier than it first appears.** When you drill offshore in the continental shelf, there are two depth axes: water depth (seabed depth) and subsurface depth below the seabed. A well in 150 m of water targeting a reservoir 3 km below the seabed is fundamentally different in cost and logistics from a well on land targeting 3 km, even though the resource is at the same subsurface stratum. I'd suggest treating water depth as a separate parameter (a modifier on cost and access) rather than trying to fold it into the strata definitions, which should reference depth below the surface (land or seabed).

**Your strata naming convention is clear but becomes somewhat ambiguous at the lower end.** "Extreme deep" and "ultra deep" could easily be confused. Some alternative naming conventions used in industry and geoscience include numerical schemes (Stratum 1 through 5), functional names (Surface, Conventional, Frontier, Speculative, Theoretical), or depth-anchored codes (S-300, S-2K, S-5K, S-10K, S-20K, where the number is the base depth). A functional naming scheme has the advantage of immediately communicating the technological accessibility of the stratum.

## Summary of Key Simplifications and Omissions

The stratification model treats depth as the primary independent variable and imposes hard boundaries where reality has gradients. In actual geology, the transitions between extraction regimes are continuous and depend on rock type, temperature, pressure, fluid conditions, and available technology — not just depth. The model as proposed does not encode lateral geological variation (basin vs. craton vs. orogen), which is critical for determining which resources exist at which depths in a given location. Thermal gradient is assumed to be roughly uniform (25–30°C/km), but in reality it varies from about 10°C/km in old, cold cratonic lithosphere to over 60°C/km in volcanically active regions and some sedimentary basins; this variation will affect which strata are accessible and what resources are stable at depth. The five-stratum model compresses the entire range of surface extraction — from topsoil scraping to massive open pits — into a single layer. The resource list omits geothermal energy, lithium brines, construction aggregates, and water. Finally, the strata model says nothing about structural geology (faults, folds, unconformities), which in reality controls where extractable concentrations of resources actually accumulate.

# Proposed Ocean Floor Strata for Deep-Ocean Resource Extraction

## Design Rationale

The fundamental difference between continental and ocean floor extraction is that the ocean strata must encode **water depth** as the primary dimension, since water depth is the dominant cost, pressure, and logistics driver for any seabed operation. On the continental strata, depth below surface determines rock pressure and temperature; on the ocean floor, the water column itself imposes massive hydrostatic pressure before you even reach the seabed. For reference, hydrostatic pressure increases at roughly 10 MPa per kilometer of water depth, so at 5 km water depth you already face ~50 MPa of ambient pressure before any sub-seabed penetration.

Most known and theorized deep-ocean mineral resources sit at or very near the seabed surface — polymetallic nodules rest directly on the seabed, cobalt-rich crusts coat seamount surfaces to thicknesses of centimeters to roughly 25 cm, seafloor massive sulfide deposits extend tens of meters into the seabed, and REE-bearing muds occupy the upper meters of abyssal sediment. This means that for the majority of ocean floor extraction scenarios, sub-seabed penetration depth is shallow enough to fall entirely within your continental "Surface" stratum (0–100 m below the seabed). I'll address the exceptions at the end.

## Recommended Four Ocean Floor Strata

**Bathyal Floor: 0.2 – 2.0 km water depth**

This stratum begins at the conventional continental shelf edge (~200 m) and extends through the continental slope and onto upper seamount features. Hydrostatic pressure ranges from roughly 2 to 20 MPa. This is the most accessible deep-ocean environment and the one closest to current technological reach. The Solwara 1 project (Nautilus Minerals, Bismarck Sea off Papua New Guinea) targeted seafloor massive sulfide deposits at approximately 1,600 m water depth, making it the first attempted commercial deep-sea mining operation, though it ultimately failed commercially and the company entered bankruptcy in 2019. Remotely operated vehicles (ROVs) for scientific and industrial work routinely operate in this depth range. Resources here include cobalt-rich ferromanganese crusts on seamounts (most crusts of economic interest are at 800–2,500 m water depth on seamount flanks and summits; Hein et al., 2013, *Ore Geology Reviews*), seafloor massive sulfide (SMS) deposits at hydrothermal vents along mid-ocean ridge segments and back-arc basins, phosphorite deposits on the upper continental slope, and gas hydrates in slope sediments. Jurisdictionally, this stratum has the most overlap with national claims — portions of the continental slope and seamounts near island nations may fall within EEZs or extended continental shelf claims under UNCLOS Article 76. Your simulation should expect that some deposits in this stratum are nationally controlled while others are in the Area (the international seabed governed by the International Seabed Authority).

**Abyssal Pelagic Floor: 2.0 – 4.0 km water depth**

This stratum covers the lower continental rise, mid-ocean ridge crests and flanks, and the shallower portions of abyssal basins. Hydrostatic pressure ranges from 20 to 40 MPa. Mid-ocean ridge crests typically sit at 2,000–3,000 m water depth (Parsons and Sclak, 1977, *Journal of Geophysical Research*; actual ridge crest depths vary with spreading rate — fast-spreading ridges like the East Pacific Rise have crests at ~2,600 m, while slow-spreading ridges like the Mid-Atlantic Ridge are at ~2,500–3,000 m with significant topographic variability). This is where the International Seabed Authority (ISA) has issued exploration contracts for SMS deposits along the Mid-Atlantic Ridge and Central and Southwest Indian Ridges. Resources include SMS deposits at deeper hydrothermal vent fields, metalliferous sediments flanking active and extinct ridge segments (enriched in Fe, Mn, Cu, Zn, and other metals), some cobalt-rich crusts on deep seamounts, and the upper edge of polymetallic nodule fields (nodule abundance generally increases with depth in the 3–5 km range). No commercial extraction has ever occurred at these depths, but scientific drilling through programs like the International Ocean Discovery Program (IODP) has operated extensively here, demonstrating that deploying riser and riserless drill systems at these depths is technically feasible for short-duration operations.

**Abyssal Plain Floor: 4.0 – 6.0 km water depth**

This stratum covers the vast abyssal plains that constitute the majority of the ocean floor by area. Hydrostatic pressure ranges from 40 to 60 MPa. The key resource province here is the Clarion-Clipperton Zone (CCZ) in the central Pacific, where polymetallic nodules carpet the seabed at water depths of roughly 4,000–5,000 m across an area of approximately 6 million km². These nodules contain manganese, nickel, copper, and cobalt in concentrations that have attracted significant exploration investment; as of 2024, the ISA had issued 19 exploration contracts in the CCZ and adjacent areas (ISA, Technical Study series). REE-enriched deep-sea muds have also been identified in the Pacific at these depths, particularly south of Japan (Kato et al., 2011, *Nature Geoscience*), with total rare earth oxide concentrations of 1,000–2,000 ppm in some deposits. Extraction at these depths requires lowering collector systems to the seabed and lifting material through 4–5 km of water column — the riser and lifting systems for this are currently in advanced prototype stages (e.g., the systems developed by Global Sea Mineral Resources and The Metals Company for CCZ nodule collection trials). This is the stratum where the first large-scale international-waters mining is most likely to occur if regulatory and economic conditions align.

**Hadal Floor: 6.0 – 11.0 km water depth**

This stratum covers ocean trenches — the deepest features on Earth's surface. Hydrostatic pressure ranges from 60 to 110 MPa, with the deepest point (Challenger Deep in the Mariana Trench) at approximately 10,935 m and ~109 MPa. Only a handful of crewed and uncrewed vehicles have ever reached hadal depths, and no resource extraction or even systematic resource surveying has been conducted. This stratum is entirely speculative for extraction purposes. Possible resources are poorly constrained: trench sediments are derived from the subducting and overriding plates and could contain reworked mineral material, and the unique geochemistry of the subduction environment could potentially concentrate certain elements, but there is no empirical evidence for economically interesting deposits. The extreme pressure environment also poses fundamental materials engineering challenges — standard steel and titanium pressure housings that work at abyssal depths may require redesign or exotic materials at hadal depths. For your simulation, this stratum could serve as a "technology frontier" layer that only becomes accessible with speculative future engineering, and its resource endowment would need to be essentially fabricated since real data does not exist.

## Sub-Seabed Penetration in the Ocean Context

For most ocean floor resources — nodules, crusts, shallow SMS deposits, REE muds — extraction involves the seabed surface and the first few meters to tens of meters of material. This falls well within the first continental stratum (Surface: 0–100 m below seabed) if you choose to apply a sub-seabed dimension to ocean extraction.

However, there are two scenarios where deeper sub-seabed penetration matters in the ocean context. The first is drilling for sub-seabed hydrocarbons beneath the deep ocean floor. While essentially all current deepwater oil and gas production occurs on continental margins within EEZs, your simulation's speculative future might include hydrocarbon exploration in abyssal settings far from continental margins. In that case, you'd have the combined challenge of (say) 4 km of water depth plus 3 km of sub-seabed drilling, and the total pressure and logistics burden would be extreme. The second is extraction from the oceanic crust itself — the basaltic and gabbroic layers that make up the 6–7 km thick oceanic crust (White et al., 1992, *Journal of Geophysical Research*). Massive sulfide mineralization can extend hundreds of meters below the seabed in some fossil hydrothermal systems, and the lower oceanic crust contains cumulate gabbros and potentially chromite-bearing ultramafic rocks above the Moho.

I'd recommend handling sub-seabed penetration as a secondary dimension applied when needed, reusing your continental Surface (0–0.1 km) and Near-Surface (0.1–0.5 km) strata below the seabed. Anything deeper than 0.5 km below the deep ocean floor is so speculative that further stratification is probably not needed in the initial model. This gives you a manageable matrix: for any ocean extraction operation, you specify the ocean floor stratum (water depth) and optionally the sub-seabed stratum (penetration depth), and the cost and technology requirements compound.

## Resource Mapping to Ocean Floor Strata

Your existing resource categories map onto the ocean floor strata in a narrower way than on the continental strata. The resources most relevant to ocean floor extraction are "industrial metals ore" (this is where nodules and crusts fit — they carry Mn, Ni, Cu, Co and don't map neatly to any single metal category), "precious metals ore" (Au, Ag, and platinum-group elements from SMS deposits and cobalt crusts), "rare earths ore" (REE-bearing abyssal muds), and "gas" (gas hydrates on continental slopes, primarily in the bathyal stratum, and potentially sub-seabed gas in deeper settings). "Industrial minerals" could encompass phosphorites from the upper slope. "Oil" is conceivable in sub-seabed plays beneath the ocean floor but is highly speculative in international waters beyond continental margins. "Brine" could represent metal-rich hydrothermal brines at vent sites — the Atlantis II Deep in the Red Sea is a well-documented example of metalliferous brine pools, though that particular site is in a nascent ocean basin rather than a mature ocean.

Resources from your list that have little or no relevance to ocean floor strata include coal (a terrestrial/shallow-marine sedimentary resource with no deep-ocean analog), aluminum ore (bauxite forms by tropical weathering of continental rocks), uranium ore (trace uranium exists in seawater and sediments but at concentrations far below what is usually meant by "ore"), stone, and regolith (though abyssal sediment could be loosely analogized to regolith, there is no plausible demand for extracting it as a bulk material). Water is obviously abundant but not a meaningful extraction target in the ocean.

## Jurisdictional Note

Your rough separation — continental strata as mainly national, ocean floor strata as mainly international — is workable but has a significant gray zone. Under UNCLOS, a coastal state's sovereign rights over seabed resources extend to 200 nautical miles (the EEZ) and potentially further under extended continental shelf claims, which can reach 350 nm or 100 nm beyond the 2,500 m isobath (UNCLOS Article 76). Several nations have filed or are pursuing extended continental shelf claims that cover substantial areas of the continental slope and rise, pushing national resource jurisdiction well into bathyal and sometimes abyssal water depths. For your simulation, I'd suggest treating the jurisdictional boundary as a separate data layer overlaid on the strata, rather than baking it into the strata definitions. The strata should be purely physical, and the national-vs-international question should be resolved by geography.

## Summary of Simplifications and Omissions

The four-stratum ocean model reduces a continuous water-depth gradient to four discrete zones and assumes that water depth is the primary cost and access variable, which is broadly true but ignores factors like ocean current regimes, seabed topography (flat abyssal plain vs. rugged ridge terrain), and distance from port (which can dominate logistics costs). The resource mapping assumes that deposit types cluster by water depth, which is a reasonable first-order approximation based on current geological understanding but may not hold for all deposit types in all ocean basins. The model does not differentiate between ocean basins (Pacific, Atlantic, Indian), which have substantially different seabed geologies, spreading rates, sediment cover, and resource endowments — the Pacific is far richer in polymetallic nodules than the Atlantic, for instance. The model omits seawater itself as a resource (dissolved uranium, lithium, and other elements in seawater have been subjects of extraction research, though none is currently economic). The model also does not address environmental and regulatory constraints, which in practice are among the strongest determinants of whether deep-sea mining occurs — the ISA's regulatory framework for exploitation in the Area remains incomplete as of early 2026.
